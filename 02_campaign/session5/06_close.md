## Must-Hit Beats

> [!important] Must Hit
> **① Fresh Concord boot prints in the mud — military standard, heading toward the clearing the party just left.** Not old. Not ambiguous. Someone was here recently, moving with purpose in the direction the party came from.

> [!important] Must Hit
> **② The "three days out" claim is revealed as false.** The Harmonic Assessment team is not three days out. They are already here. They have already been to the Listening Rock. The party is not ahead of anything. They are behind it.

## Optional Beats

> [!tip] Optional — use if the scene has room
> - A **second set of prints** — lighter, spaced differently — suggests the team split up at some point. Not all of them went to the clearing. Some went elsewhere. Where? The party doesn't know. You don't have to know yet either.
> - A **discarded piece of equipment** near the prints — a small glass measuring vial, Concord-standard issue, empty, the kind Vision scholars use for atmospheric sampling. It was not there on the way out. It was left recently. Possibly intentionally.
> - The **birds go quiet again** — briefly, in the direction of the clearing, then return. Something disturbed them. The party just left. Whatever disturbed them is still back there, or just left.

## DM Prep Notes

> [!warning] DM Only
> - **This scene is a single beat, not a conversation.** The boot prints are the session's final punctuation mark. Do not pad it. Do not follow it with exposition. Read the aloud, describe the prints, let the players react for thirty seconds, and end the session. The power of the hook is in what it doesn't explain.
> - **The Assessment team has four members:** three Vision scholars (cover identity) and one Might escort (unlisted on the manifest). The scholars are real Vision operatives, not entirely unwilling participants in the deception — they know they are here under Guile's authority but believe their work is legitimate scholarship. The escort is Guile's agent. He makes the decisions. The scholars measure things and do not ask questions.
> - **What the team did at the Listening Rock:** They were there before the party arrived. They ran a passive observation — instruments only, no contact with Mira, no attempt at dampening this visit. They were there to confirm what their instruments in Orrenval had already flagged: an anomalous resonance event at this location. They got their confirmation. What the instruments flagged was the terrarium sync — or rather, the precursor to it. Oscar's proximity to the stone was already registering before the sync itself. The escort filed a report the moment the scholars finished. That report is now travelling back to Orrenval faster than the party can walk.
> - **The "three days out" lie** was not Pell's lie. Pell was given false information, believed it, and passed it on. The lie originated with whoever drafted the warning letter — the Free Peoples contact in Fenwick Crossing. They knew the team was already in the Ashroot when they wrote "three days." The letter was not a warning. It was a test of how the party would respond to the news — whether they would run, investigate, or do exactly what they did: go to the lower roots and the resonance site. Someone is watching the party's choices. This thread is yours to develop. It does not need to resolve this session.
> - **Do not let the party chase the team tonight.** The team is professionals moving quickly in territory they have already scouted. The party has been underground, in combat, and walking for hours. If the party attempts pursuit, the trail goes cold within twenty minutes — the team knows how to move without being followed. The session ends on the discovery, not the chase.
> - **What comes next** (for your session 6 planning): The party must decide whether to report to the Council of 8 first or attempt to locate the Assessment team first. Both are valid. Both have costs. The Council may already know the team is here — "We already know" is their register. The Assessment team, for their part, is not yet certain the party is significant. The escort has a description of a warforged construct from the Dawnswell alert. He does not yet have confirmation it is Oscar. He will.

## The Boot Prints — What They Look Like

> [!quote] Read Aloud — The Discovery
> The path back to Rootspire is familiar now — you've walked it twice, the light has gone soft and amber in the canopy overhead, the birdsong has come back around you. Your own footprints from the outward journey are visible in the softer sections of the path, pressed into the mud from this morning.
>
> You notice them because they are on top of yours.
>
> Fresh prints. Military-standard sole — the kind of deep, even tread that comes from boots made for long distances and hard ground. Four sets, overlapping slightly, moving in the same direction: toward the clearing you just left.
>
> They are not from this morning. The mud at their edges hasn't had time to dry.
>
> They were made while you were at the stone.

*Stop here. Let the party react. Give them thirty seconds — a minute at most. Then:*

> [!quote] Read Aloud — The Closer Look
> One set of prints is heavier than the others — deeper heel impression, longer stride. The others are even, measured, the prints of people who have been walking a long time and have found their pace. All four sets continue past you on the path and do not return.
>
> They did not come back this way.
>
> Three days out.

*End session.*

## Running the Final Beat

The rhythm of this scene matters as much as its content. Here is the beat-by-beat:

**Step 1 — establish the walk back.** One or two sentences of normalcy. The light is good. The party is tired but intact. The session has been long and heavy and they are walking home. Let them breathe.

**Step 2 — the incidental discovery.** Do not announce that something significant is about to happen. The party notices the prints because they are looking at the path — or because whoever is in front glances down. Frame it as an observation, not a revelation. *You notice them because they are on top of yours.*

**Step 3 — let the realisation land.** Give the party the physical details: four sets, military sole, fresh, heading toward the clearing. Do not editorialise. Do not tell them what this means. They know what this means.

**Step 4 — the callback.** *Three days out.* Three words. The letter from Scene 01, echoed here. The full weight of Pell's tells — the satchel strap, the pause — landing retroactively. The party was behind the Concord from the moment the session started. They just didn't know it.

**Step 5 — end the session.** Do not give the party time to take action. Do not open a discussion. Ask if there are any immediate reactions — let each player say one thing if they want — and then close. The hook is not a problem to solve tonight. It is a door that will open next session.

> [!warning] DM Only
> If a player immediately says "I follow the prints" — have them lose the trail quickly, within ten minutes, in fading light on unfamiliar ground. The team is already gone. Award the instinct, deny the outcome, end the session on the loss of the trail rather than the discovery of the prints if that is more dramatically satisfying for your table. Either version works. The emotional note — we are behind, we did not see this coming, something is watching us — is the same.

## The Final Image

If you want a single closing image to hold in mind as you end the session — something to describe in the last ten seconds before you call it:

> [!quote] Read Aloud — Optional Closing Image
> The path back to Rootspire stretches ahead of you in the fading light. The Great Tree is visible above the canopy now — you are close enough to see it again, enormous and dark against the early evening sky.
>
> Behind you: a clearing where something very old breathes every three minutes, whether or not anyone is there to feel it.
>
> Underneath you: a set of boot prints that were not there this morning, heading back the way you came.
>
> Somewhere between those two things — in the Ashroot, in the dark, in the space between what you know and what you don't — four people are moving quickly and quietly, and they know exactly where they are going.

## Threads This Scene Closes — and Opens

- **The ticking clock** — established in Scene 01, paid off here. The clock was never three days. It was already running when the session started. This retroactively reframes the entire session: every choice the party made, the Concord was one step ahead.
- **The Concord threat** — moves from background tension to foreground reality. The Assessment team is not an abstraction anymore. They have soles and strides and a weight to their footsteps. They were thirty feet from where the party stood, within the last two hours.
- **Oscar's arc** — the terrarium sync in Scene 05 may have already flagged Oscar to the Assessment team's instruments. Oscar does not know this. If they told the party everything in Scene 04, the party doesn't know this either — yet. If they told the party nothing, Oscar alone may eventually understand the implication. Either way, the Concord is now, functionally, looking for Oscar. They just don't have a name or a face to attach to the anomalous reading.
- **The observer problem** — the party was sent to observe. They observed. What they could not observe was that they were also being observed. The Council of 8's instruction — "observe, don't intervene" — protected the party from taking action that would have revealed them more fully. Following the instruction was, accidentally, the correct tactical move. This is worth noting. The Council may have known.
