## Must-Hit Beats

> [!important] Must Hit
> **① Oscar finds the crystalline housing inside Vessel-Null's chest cavity.** It mirrors the core mechanism of Oscar's terrarium. This is a discovery, not an explanation — do not over-narrate it. Let Oscar's player sit with what they are looking at.

> [!important] Must Hit
> **② The question shifts.** "What am I?" becomes "What was I made to be?" — and underneath that, unspoken: "Does that have to be what I am?" These questions should be present in the room without being stated aloud by the DM.

> [!important] Must Hit
> **③ The level-up is called here.** Framed as transformation — every character in this room just survived something difficult and learned something they cannot unlearn. The world recognises them differently now.

## Optional Beats

> [!tip] Optional — use if the scene has room
> - Oscar finds **Vessel-Null's serial designation** etched inside the chest cavity — a partial string of characters, partially obscured by crystal growth. Not legible in full, but enough to suggest a naming convention: a prefix that Oscar may recognise as similar to their own designation. A breadcrumb, not a revelation.
> - **Oscar's player decides privately** what to tell the party about what they found — all of it, some of it, none of it. Give them a genuine moment to make that choice. It is the first real test of who Oscar is becoming.
> - **Each of the other PCs has a quiet beat** while Oscar processes — a moment of stillness that belongs to their own inner life. Suggestions in the PC Moments table below.
> - **The terrarium responds** to the crystalline housing when they are brought close together — the plant inside goes completely still, then slowly, deliberately, turns to face the housing. Not distress. Recognition.

## DM Prep Notes

> [!warning] DM Only
> - This scene is not an action scene. It is an interior scene. Your job is to create the conditions for Oscar's player to feel the weight of what they've found and then protect the silence that follows. Resist the urge to fill it.
> - **Do not answer the drift question here.** Vessel-Null asked it. Oscar carries it. That is enough for this session. If Oscar's player asks you directly — "does Oscar have the drift?" — say: "Oscar doesn't know yet." Because they don't.
> - **The crystalline housing is not a weapon, a key, or a MacGuffin.** It is a piece of evidence. It tells Oscar — and by extension the party — that Oscar's terrarium is not a feature of their design. It is a symptom of their function. Oscar was built to contain something. The terrarium is what that containment looks like from the outside when it is working.
> - **What Oscar chooses to share with the party is genuinely consequential.** If they share everything, the party has context for Scene 05's terrarium sync moment. If they share nothing, that moment lands differently — more isolated, more loaded. Either choice is correct. Do not steer it.
> - **The level-up frame matters.** This is not a mechanical pause in the story. It is the story acknowledging that something has changed. Frame it that way in your narration — briefly, then step back and let the players do their level-up work.
> - **Vessel-Null's body.** The party must decide what to do with it. They can leave it, carry it, examine it further. There is no correct answer and no time pressure. What they choose says something about them. Note it.

## The Discovery — Oscar's Chest Cavity Find

Oscar notices it, or someone examining Vessel-Null's chassis notices it and defers to Oscar. Either approach works.

> [!quote] Read Aloud — The Housing
> The chassis has cracked open along a seam in the chest — impact damage from the fight, or the growth finally splitting what it had been working at for years. Inside, past the mechanisms you'd expect — the regulators, the armature connectors, the basic construction of something built to move and hold a shape — there is a secondary chamber.
>
> Small. Deliberate. Not load-bearing in any mechanical sense. It was built to hold something specific, the way a reliquary is built: not for function, for preservation.
>
> Inside it: a crystalline housing, roughly the size of a closed fist. Translucent. The crystal growth has infiltrated it — tendrils through the housing's lattice — but the housing itself is intact. And the structure of it, the architecture of the chamber it sits in, the way it is oriented in the chassis —
>
> It is the same as the terrarium. Not similar. The same principle, the same orientation, the same internal logic, rendered in a different material at a different scale.
>
> Not a copy. A predecessor.

*Give Oscar's player this moment. Let them speak first. Let them decide what they do — examine it, take it, leave it, touch it. Do not rush past their reaction.*

## The Terrarium Response

*If Oscar brings the terrarium near the housing — or if the terrarium is simply in the room, which it is:*

> [!quote] Read Aloud — Terrarium Recognition
> The plant in the terrarium has been still since you entered the Hollow Nave. Not dormant — still. The particular stillness of something waiting.
>
> When the housing is near it — or when Oscar is near it, holding the housing, or simply standing above the opened chassis — the plant moves. Slowly. A deliberate rotation, the way a sunflower moves, but there is no sun here. It turns to face the housing and stops.
>
> The terrarium does not glow. It does not pulse. It simply — orients. The way it oriented on the descent, pointing toward something it recognised.
>
> It recognised this. It has always recognised this. It just had to get close enough.

> [!warning] DM Only
> The terrarium is not sentient. It does not make decisions. What the party is observing is a resonance response — the terrarium's core mechanism (containment, processing, stabilisation of divine resonance) recognising a structurally identical mechanism nearby and aligning with it. Like two tuning forks. This is not magic in the traditional sense. It is physics. Deeply strange physics, but physics. Oscar does not know this yet. The party does not know this yet. But a DC 16 Arcana check by any character will surface the tuning-fork framework — two resonance mechanisms recognising each other.

## PC Moments — The Quiet While Oscar Processes

*These are brief, optional moments for the other characters during the scene's stillness. Use one or two — not all of them. They should feel like breathing space, not spotlight theft.*

> [!tip] Zennor
> The worn stone circle at the chamber's centre — foot-worn over centuries by people coming to this place with something to offer or ask. Zennor, if he stands in it, finds it fits him without trying. Not a trap. Not a sign. Just a place where people have stood and felt the weight of things. He knows that feeling. He's been standing in it for years.

> [!tip] Hog
> The Blighted creatures have dissolved back into the root-growth. One section of root-wall where they emerged has left a gap — and inside that gap, nestled in the root mass, is a stone the size of a fist, perfectly smooth, perfectly round, with no natural explanation for how it got there. It is warm. It hums faintly when Hog holds it. It is almost certainly nothing significant. It is almost certainly something he should keep.
> *(DM: this is a small mystery you can develop or discard. The stone is a minor resonance-saturated object — no mechanical effect yet. Hog's chaotic energy is the right kind of magnet for small unexplained things.)*

> [!tip] Fergie
> The crystalline growth on Vessel-Null's chassis is dimming — losing its light as the resonance drains away. But near the joints, where the crystal was thickest, the dying light has a colour Fergie recognises: ember-orange. The same colour as the dark on the descent. Whatever lives in fire — whatever Fergie has been reaching toward — it left a mark here too. Fire was here, or something adjacent to fire, in whatever this construct was holding.

## The Level-Up Moment

When the scene has breathed — when Oscar has found the housing, when the party has had their quiet — this is the moment.

Do not announce it mechanically. Frame it first.

> [!quote] Read Aloud — Level-Up Frame
> There is a stillness in this chamber that feels different from the silence you descended through. That was the quiet of something waiting. This is the quiet of something that has just happened — the particular hush that follows a door opening, when the air from two rooms is still deciding how to mix.
>
> You are not the same people who came down the stairs. You've fought something that didn't want to fight you. You've heard a question that has no easy answer. You've stood in a place that was tended for thirty years by someone above who never knew exactly what she was tending toward — and now you do, or the edge of it, which is its own kind of weight.
>
> Something in the world has noticed. Take a moment. You've earned it.

*Call the level-up. Give the table whatever time they need. This is not a pause in the story — it is the story marking itself.*

> [!warning] DM Only
> While the players handle level-up mechanics, stay present at the table. This is a good time for quiet side conversations — check in with Oscar's player specifically. Ask them, quietly and without pressure: "What does Oscar do with the housing?" and "What does Oscar tell the party?" These are not questions you need answers to right now. They are questions Oscar's player should be holding as they level up.
>
> Frame each character's level-up in the fiction briefly when you resume:
> - **Zennor** — harder. Not angrier. Something has settled that was restless. He moves through the chamber with the quiet authority of someone who has stopped arguing with what he is.
> - **Hog** — faster, sharper, more himself. Whatever Hog is — chaotic, instinctive, surprisingly formidable — he is more of it now. The fight in the Hollow Nave reminded him that he is very good at being Hog.
> - **Fergie** — the fire is different. Not stronger necessarily, but more knowing. Something in the chamber recognised Fergie's fire and Fergie felt it. The flame feels like it has a direction now, even when it doesn't.
> - **Oscar** — holds something they didn't hold before. Not just the housing. Knowledge of a specific shape — the shape of what they were built to be — and the first real understanding that being built for something and choosing something are not the same sentence.

## Oscar's Choice — What to Tell the Party

*This is the scene's most important long-term beat. Handle it with care.*

Oscar now knows — or strongly suspects — that:
- They were built as a containment vessel for divine resonance
- Their terrarium is not a tool they carry but a mechanism they are
- Vessel-Null is what they could become if the containment drifts
- Someone called this Project Dawnswell and built at least two iterations

What Oscar chooses to share with the party is a genuine character decision. Do not make it for them. Do not prompt a specific answer. Simply ask Oscar's player, when the moment is right:

*"What does Oscar tell the rest of the party about what they found?"*

Then listen. Whatever they say — everything, something, nothing — is the right answer for Oscar. Note it. It will matter.

> [!warning] DM Only
> If Oscar shares everything: the party enters Scene 05 with context for the terrarium sync moment. When the terrarium pulses in time with the Listening Rock, they understand what they're seeing. The moment lands as confirmation.
>
> If Oscar shares nothing, or very little: the terrarium sync in Scene 05 lands in isolation — Oscar alone understands what is happening, and the party sees something strange without the framework to read it. This is more dramatically loaded and slightly more painful. Both versions are good. The second is arguably better.
>
> If Oscar shares some but not all: Oscar is already making decisions about identity and self-presentation. Who Oscar chooses to be in front of the party is the beginning of who Oscar will become. This is the version most likely to generate meaningful inter-party roleplay in future sessions.

## Vessel-Null's Body — What Does the Party Do?

There is no correct answer. There is no time pressure. But the choice should be made consciously.

| If the party... | Then... |
|---|---|
| Leave Vessel-Null in the Hollow Nave | It stays. Maren will find it on her next visit and understand, in her way, what happened here. She will tend it as she tends everything else. |
| Carry Vessel-Null out | Heavy — a construct chassis is not light. But possible with effort. If they bring it to Rootspire, it becomes a complicated object: evidence, artefact, and body all at once. The Council of 8 will have opinions. |
| Examine it further before leaving | The DM can surface the serial designation check (DC 15) here if it hasn't come up yet. The housing markings check (DC 13) as well. Reward thoroughness without padding. |
| Attempt to reactivate it | Not possible. The chassis failure is complete. The resonance that animated it has dissipated. What remains is machinery. Beautiful, terrible machinery. |
| Ask Oscar what they want to do with it | This is the right question. Oscar's answer is the scene's final beat, whatever it is. Let it be the last thing said before the party begins the climb back up. |

## Transitioning Out

The scene ends when the party decides to surface. There is no DM push needed — the level-up, the quiet, and the weight of the chamber will move the party toward the exit naturally.

> [!quote] Read Aloud — Beginning the Climb
> The way out is the way you came. The passage feels different going up — narrower somehow, the dark less attentive. As if whatever was watching on the descent has turned its attention elsewhere, or gone back to whatever it was doing before you interrupted it.
>
> The offerings in the hollows are still there. Undisturbed. The candle stubs, the carved figures, the knotted cloth. Everything exactly as Maren left it.
>
> Halfway up, without deciding to, all of you look back down. The Hollow Nave's light — Vessel-Null's light — is gone. There is only dark below now.
>
> Then the dark pulses. Once. Slowly. And is still.

*Move to Scene 05.*

> [!warning] If This Scene Runs Short
> 15 minutes is the floor, not the target. If the table is moving fast, slow down here. Ask Oscar's player directly what Oscar is feeling. Ask Zennor what he sees when he looks at Vessel-Null's body. Give the scene the time it has earned. The only scene you should cut time from to protect this one is Scene 02 — never Scene 03, never Scene 05.

## Threads This Scene Advances

- **Oscar's arc** — The housing discovery, the terrarium response, and the choice about what to share are the three structural pillars of Oscar's story. All three are now in play. Everything in the back half of the campaign that touches Oscar's identity runs through what happened in this chamber.
- **Project Dawnswell** — The party now has fragments: a name, a numeral, an architectural pattern. Not enough to act on. Enough to ask questions. The Concord Assessment team walking toward Rootspire is here because of Dawnswell. The party doesn't know that yet.
- **The Veil's nature** — The containment mechanism at Oscar's core is, in miniature, the same principle as the Veil Core beneath the Sanctara. A vessel built to hold something divine that doesn't understand restraint. The party is walking around with a pocket version of the thing the Concord has built its entire civilisation on top of. They do not know this yet. The DM does.
- **Inter-party dynamics** — What Oscar chooses to share creates a new social texture in the party going into Scene 05 and beyond. Watch for it. Reflect it back.
