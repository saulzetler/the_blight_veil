## 🎭 1. Designing Distinct Voices

The best voices are built from **two or three strong anchors**, not full impersonations.  
Here’s how to design them quickly and sustainably:

### A. Start with Three Anchors

Pick one from each category below — the combination gives you a unique feel.

|Anchor Type|Examples|Quick Shortcut|
|---|---|---|
|**Pitch / Placement**|High (nasal), Mid (neutral), Low (chest or throat)|Say “hmm…” and notice _where_ your voice vibrates.|
|**Pace / Rhythm**|Slow & deliberate, clipped & military, lilting & musical, rambling|Read one sentence of dialogue both too fast and too slow to find the middle.|
|**Texture / Energy**|Gravelly, breathy, smooth, stern, tired, sing-song|Use one emotion or physical trait to justify it — “smoker,” “scholar,” “noble,” “hunter.”|

> **Example**: Iria Cael, your pragmatic field medic.
> 
> - **Pitch:** Mid, soft in the mouth.
>     
> - **Pace:** Quick but measured.
>     
> - **Texture:** Dry and slightly hoarse.  
>     → Result: “Soft-spoken but with grit — like someone who’s seen too much and won’t waste words.”
>     

---

### B. Create a Physical Trigger

Every NPC should have a _gesture_ or _posture_ cue that brings the voice back.  
Examples:

- Concord captain → shoulders back, chin up.
    
- Cindersong rebel → lean forward, half-smile.
    
- Old priest → hands clasped, head tilted down.
    

Before you speak as them, slip into that _body cue_ for half a second — it resets your tone without thinking.

---

### C. Write a One-Line “Voice Note”

Keep a 3–6 word note on each major NPC in your prep or initiative tracker:

> **Iria Cael** – “Low, dry, steady; clipped healer”  
> **Captain Deyran** – “Loud, righteous, brittle under pressure”  
> **Vaereth** – “Velvet tone, coiled like smoke”

This is enough to remind you of their vibe on the fly.

## 🗣️ 2. Performing During Session

### A. Use “Sliding Accents,” Not Fixed Ones

You don’t need to maintain a full Scottish or French accent — just hint at rhythm and vowel shapes.

- _Scottish-inspired:_ round your “o”s (“nooo”), tap your “r”s.
    
- _French-inspired:_ soften “th” to “z” and shorten vowels.  
    If you _suggest_ the sound at the start of their dialogue, your players will fill in the rest.
    

### B. Open with a Catchphrase or “Reset Line”

Each time you return to an NPC, start with a familiar phrase in their voice to lock it in:

- “By the dawn’s light…” (Priest)
    
- “You lot again, eh?” (Guard)
    
- “Quiet, now—breathe.” (Iria)
    

This reminds _you_ who they are as much as it reminds the players.

### C. Exaggerate Emotion, Not Sound

If an NPC is angry, make their sentences shorter.  
If they’re tired, drag out the vowels.  
Emotion shapes the delivery more than accent — and it’s much easier to sustain.

---

## 🔁 3. Keeping Consistency Over Time

### A. Record a Reference Line

Before a session, spend 15 seconds recording yourself saying a line in the character’s voice.  
Label them (“Iria Cael – calm.mp3”) — listen to it before your next game. It’s your “voice key.”

### B. Use Tags on Your Prep Sheets

On your NPC sheets, include:

```
Voice: husky alto | Gesture: hand over chest | Reset line: “Steady, now.”
```

Read that before you speak, and you’ll automatically click back into the right mode.

### C. Don’t Panic About Drift

If a voice drifts mid-session, let it.  
The consistency of _attitude_ and _word choice_ will do more than any pitch or accent precision.

---

## 🧠 4. Optional Advanced Trick — “The 2-Step Filter”

When creating or recalling a voice, think of:

1. **Who are they emotionally right now?**  
    (Tired, cautious, zealous, charming?)
    
2. **Where do they speak from physically?**  
    (Chest, throat, nose, teeth?)
    

Those two answers give you a unique tone without overthinking performance.
