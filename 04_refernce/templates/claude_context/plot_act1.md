# Plotting Act One — Campaign Structure Reference

> [!warning] DM Reference Only Summarized from: _"Plotting a Campaign Like a Pro — Act One"_ (YouTube). Use this as a structural framework when planning new arcs or sessions for The Blight Veil.

---

## Core Philosophy

Think of your campaign like a **novel or film**, not a series of encounters. Players experience the story as a theater of the mind — they're watching an internal movie. Your job is to give them scenes, not just stat blocks.

- Paint in **broad strokes** early. A small, detailed starting location beats a half-built world.
- Avoid **exposition dumps**. Reveal the world naturally through play.
- The intro exists to establish **tone, vibe, and DM style** — not to deliver lore.

---

## The Five Steps of Act One

### Step 1 — Establish the Setting

Pick a starting location with just enough detail to invite exploration. Leave most of it shrouded in mystery.

- Give players a **taste**, not a full course.
- Communicate the **tone and atmosphere** of the world.
- Have a prepared intro — it doesn't need to be scripted, but it should pull them in.

---

### Step 2 — Define the Inciting Incident

Ask: _Why are we telling this story right now? What makes this moment special?_

The inciting incident is the **story's ignition** — the moment things stop being normal.

- Should land at the **end of Session 1** for maximum impact.
- Can be existential (world-ending threat), personal (crime boss, vendetta), or natural (plague, disaster).
- Does **not** need to connect directly to the BBEG yet — you can retrofit that later.
- Begin thinking about the BBEG now: _What do they want? What steps do they need to take?_

> [!tip] Frodo Principle The "normal world" is your intro roleplay. The inciting incident is the moment Frodo is told to leave the Shire. Don't wait too long to pull the trigger.

---

### Step 3 — Plot the Trail of Trials

A sequence of escalating encounters and challenges between the inciting incident and the point of no return.

Structure as **Trial A → Trial B → Trial C** (add more for a longer act):

|Trial|Purpose|
|---|---|
|A|First real danger; players learn the threat is real|
|B|Stakes escalate; skills are tested under pressure|
|C|Confrontation or threshold moment; seeds BBEG hints|

- Each trial should **raise stakes** progressively.
- Drop **early BBEG hints**: spooky voices, visions, masked figures, rumours — never too much.
- Encounters should feel **connected** to the central conflict, not random.

---

### Step 4 — Weave in PC Backstories

Backstories are threads. Use them.

- Tie at least one or two character backstories to the central conflict by end of Act One.
- Use **NPCs as backstory prompts**: a mysterious doctor, a pursuing enemy, a familiar name spoken by a dying villain.
- Session Zero is your best tool — ask players how their characters connect to the world and pre-plan hooks.
- Players should learn about each other _through play_, not through monologues.

> [!tip] The Retrofit Move If a player isn't biting on the main hook, connect their backstory to it. Lost sibling? They're involved. Running from the law? The law is here. Seeking an artifact? It's on the island. Find the lever.

---

### Step 5 — The Point of No Return

The moment that **locks the party into the story**. Stakes become personal. The path forward is clear.

- Should feel **life-altering** — not just plot-advancing.
- Ideal moment to make a direct backstory connection (a name, a sigil, a face they recognise).
- The larger world now knows who the party is — factions take notice.
- Players are no longer random adventurers. They are **chosen by circumstance**.

> [!important] The Test By the end of Act One, players should feel: anchored in the world, personally invested in the conflict, and compelled to keep pulling the thread. If they don't feel that, the point of no return hasn't landed yet.

---

## Pacing Tools

Use these between major beats to manage session length and emotional rhythm:

- **Random encounters** — buy time, add texture, relieve prep pressure
- **Lighthearted episodes** — festivals, shopping, downtime; release tension between heavy moments
- **NPC relationships** — characters players grow to love slow-burn the world into life
- **Rumours and town criers** — drop larger plot hints without direct exposition

---

## Act One Structure at a Glance

```
[Intro / Normal World]
        ↓
[Inciting Incident] ← end of Session 1
        ↓
[Trial A] → [Trial B] → [Trial C]
        ↓
[Backstory Threads Activate]
        ↓
[Point of No Return]
        ↓
[Act Two Begins]
```

---

## Application Notes for The Blight Veil

**Campaign pacing note:** Act One is projected to run 8–10 sessions. At Session 5, the party is solidly in the **Trail of Trials** phase. Nothing is locked in yet — this is mid-act escalation territory, not the endgame of Act One.

- The **Veil** itself functions as the inciting incident layer — the resonance events are Act One's Trail of Trials.
- The **Council of 8's mandate** (observe the resonance sites) is a classic Trial A structure — low stakes, high mystery.
- Oscar's arc (Vessel-Null, construct identity) is a natural **Step 4 backstory activation** — currently opening up, not resolved.
- The **Concord's arrival at the resonance sites** (Session 5 closing hook) is a **mid-act escalation** — Trial B or C territory. It raises stakes without closing doors. The party can still operate with some distance from the collision.
- **Three resonance sites remain** — each can function as its own trial, with the Concord's presence growing more entangled at each one.
- **Zennor's grief thread** (wife, daughter, guilt) is an untriggered backstory hook — still waiting for the right NPC, vision, or moment to activate it.
- The **Point of No Return** has not yet been identified — worth deciding early so the Trail of Trials has a clear target to aim at.
